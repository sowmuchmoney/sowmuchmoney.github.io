A Different Giving Tree

Many of us remember Shel Silverstein's "The Giving Tree" being read to us when we were younger. It tells the story of a young man who loved playing near an old tree but as he grew older he no longer had time for the tree. He began visiting only when he needed something from the tree - apples to sell, branches for a home, wood for a boat, and finally a stump upon which to rest his aging bones.

Imagine a parallel universe where instead of taking from the tree, the boy cared for the tree in a manner that enabled him to use the resources it provided indefinitely. A story in which the boy planted seeds taken from the tree and grew more trees. A story of stewardship instead of generosity, a relationship where both parties grew closer instead of the infrequent visits that grew further apart as time went on. What does that look like? Does it look like a better story that ends with an elderly man sitting in the shade of his orchard instead of on a weathered stump in an abandoned lot?


The first version is how most Americans view their retirement savings and personal wealth. They build it up to one day cut it down. The second story could be the story of your retirement, one that lasts indefinitely, one that continues to grow.

Assets are like planting a tree, you water it for years and then one day it doesn't need you anymore. It's roots are deep enough and then the tree can provide shade for your enjoyment forever.
